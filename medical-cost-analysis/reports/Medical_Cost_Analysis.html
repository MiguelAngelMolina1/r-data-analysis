Medical Cost and Personal Lifestyle

Introduction

Healthcare costs are a major concern in both individual and policy making contexts. The dataset under study provides detailed information on health insurance charges for a sample of individuals. By analyzing lifestyle and demographic characteristics, we gain the opportunity to explore how personal health translates to a cost. This dataset is from Kaggle at the link below

https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/mirichoi0218/insurance/discussion?sort=hotness

In this project, I will perform an exploratory analysis to uncover relationships and patterns in this dataset. After an exploratory analysis I will then build a linear regression model with the aim at predicting insurance charges. The outcome of this analysis will be both predictive and inferential from a series of translations and visualization, thereby providing actionable insights into cost drivers within health insurance.

Focus

The main objective of this project is to understand how lifestyle and demographic characteristics affect medical insurance costs. This analysis aims to answer four main questions:

  • Which variables are the strongest predictors in medical insurance charges?

  • How much does smoking impact medical cost compared to nonsmokers?

  • Does any variable have a linear relationship with insurance costs?

  • Which variables best predict medical insurance charges, and accurately can a regression model explain variations in cost?

Description of the Dataset

This dataset contains information on individual lifestyle and demographic characteristics for 1338 observations. Each row in the dataset corresponds with one individual. The dataset includes several variables commonly associatred with health insurance pricing. The columns in the dataset are:

  • Age - age of primary beneficiary

  • Sex - insurance contractor gender, female, male

  • BMI - Body mass index, providing an understanding of body, weights that are relatively high or low relative to height, objective index of body weight ideally 18.5 to 24.9

  • Children - Number of children covered by health insurance / Number of dependents

  • Smoker - Smoking

  • Region - the beneficiary’s residential area in the US, northeast, southeast, southwest, northwest.

  • Charges - Individual medical costs billed by health insurance

These variables can be used to explore how personal characteristics influence medical costs and to build predictive regression models.

Standard Linear Regression

In a regression model, there are key values to look for when conducting an analysis:

  • Estimate represents how much the predicted medical charges change when a given variable increases by one unit, holding all other variables constant.

  • Standard Error indicates how precise that estimate is, smaller values suggest the model is more confident in that estimate.

  • t-value measures the strength of the relationship between a variable and the outcome by comparing the estimate to its standard error; a larger t-value means stronger evidence of a meaningful effect.

  • p-value tells us whether the variable has a statistically significant impact on charges, with values below 0.05 indicating a significant effect.

  • Residuals represent the differences between predicted and actual medical charges, and their spread helps us assess how well the model fits the data.

  • Residual Standard Error shows the average prediction error size, while the R-squared value tells us what percentage of variation in medical charges is explained by the model — higher values indicate better predictive performance.

The residuals represent the difference between the actual medical charges and the values predicted by the regression model. The distribution shows that most prediction errors fall between -$2,848 and +$1,395, indicating that the model performs reasonably well for a majority of individuals.

The median residual is slightly negative at -$982, suggesting a slight over prediction on overage. However, the maximum residual of nearly +$30,000 shows that there are some patients whose medical costs are far beyond the models prediction, likely due to an extreme high risk profile. Give this large variation in residuals, this model has room for improvement.

Coefficient Summary:

The coefficient estimates measure the expected change in annual medical charges for a one unit increase in each predictor, holding all other variables constant. Smoking status has by far the largest effectm, with smokers incurring approximately $23,848 more in medical charges than non smokers on average.

This is a highly significant and practically important increase. Age and BMI also have statistically significant positive effect, with charges increasing by about $257 per additional year of age and $339 per additional BMI unit. The number of children shows an impact of about $476 per additional child, suggesting limited relevance like region and sex.

In Contrast, sex does not significantly influence charges, with a p value of 0.69, and the regional effects are relatively small and inconsistent. Given regional location and sex have high p values, this indicates lifestyle and health related factors are primary drivers of medical spending. This is especially true when the p values are below 0.05 with age, BMI, and smoking.

Model Fit Summary:

The Residual Standard Error (RSE) is the average prediction error. This factor shows how far predictions are from their true charges, and for a variance of 75% the model is only off by +-$6062. The Reduced Linear Regression Model includes only three key predictors of medical charges: age, BMI, and smoking status. All variables remain highly statistically significant (p < 2e-16), indicating strong evidence that each one influences medical spending. Smoking has the largest impact, with smokers paying an estimated $23,824 more per year than non-smokers. Increases in age and BMI are also associated with higher charges, adding approximately $259 and $323 respectively for each additional unit.

The model explains 74.75% of the variation in medical charges (R² = 0.7475), which is only slightly lower than the full model that included additional predictors. The Residual Standard Error of $6,092 shows that prediction errors remain similar in magnitude to those from the full model, indicating that removing sex, region, and number of children did not result in a meaningful loss of accuracy.

Overall, the reduced model is a more efficient and interpretative model that preserves strong predictive performance while focusing on the most impactful health-related variables.

Focus Questions Answered

The main objective of this project was to understand how lifestyle and demographic characteristics affect medical insurance costs. After this analysis and applying the dataset to a Linear Regression Model in multiple ways:

  • Which variables are the strongest predictors in medical insurance charges?

    • The most influential predictors in this dataset are age, BMI, and smoking. These predictors represented the greatest influential factors contributing to medical charges with p values nearly at zero. Analyzing these predictors by a per unit margin, age showcased a $257 increase per unit of age, a $339 increase per unit of bmi, and a $23,000+ charge if individuals are smokers.

  • How much does smoking impact medical cost compared to nonsmokers?

    • Smoking is the primary driver of medical cost in this dataset, and the model estimated that smokers incur over $23,000 more compared to that of a nonsmoker. This dramatic difference suggest smoking is the most important behavioral factor influencing medical costs in this dataset.

  • Does any variable have a linear relationship with insurance costs?

    • There were two variables that had a prominent linear relationship with insurance cost. This distinction was strong and clear when analyzing scatter plots, and the strength of their coefficients. These two variables were Age, and BMI, both of which showed a positive linear associated in a per unit margin. These findings align with medical expectations as greater age and NMI correlate to increased health risks and as a result more medical charges.

  • Which variables best predict medical insurance charges, and accurately can a regression model explain variations in cost?

    • The variables that best predict medical insurance charges are smoking, BMI, and age. The reduced linear regression model explained 74.75% of the variation in medical charges (R² = 0.7475), with all predictors highly significant (p < 2e-16). Smoking produced the largest effect, increasing annual charges by more than $23,800, while BMI and age contributed moderate linear increases. LASSO regression confirmed these findings by selecting the same three predictors and shrinking sex and region coefficients to zero. Overall, age, BMI, and smoking are the dominant drivers of medical cost in this dataset.

Conclusion

In summary, this analysis shows that medical insurance charges are primarily driven by three key factors: smoking status, BMI, and age. The reduced linear regression model explained nearly 75% of the variation in medical costs (R² = 0.7475), with all three predictors showing strong statistical significance. Smoking had the largest impact, increasing charges by more than $23,000, while both age and BMI demonstrated clear linear relationships with cost. LASSO regression confirmed these findings by selecting the same predictors and shrinking less informative variables, such as sex and region, toward zero. Overall, the results indicate that lifestyle and health-related variables are far more important than demographic characteristics in determining medical insurance expenses.
